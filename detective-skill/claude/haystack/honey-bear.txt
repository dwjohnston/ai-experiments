In the sun-warmed forest of the south lived a small bear known to everyone as Honey.
He had earned the name honestly — there was nothing in the world he loved more than honey.

Honey could smell a beehive from three miles away. He knew which hollow oaks held wild hives,
which cliff faces had cracks where bees nested, and which farmers kept painted wooden hives
at the edges of their orchards.

His great weakness was patience. He would plunge his paw in too fast, disturb the bees,
and spend the afternoon running with a cloud of angry insects behind him. Yet every morning
he would set out again, hopeful and determined.

One spring he discovered a hive so enormous it filled an entire fallen oak. The honey inside
was dark and thick, flavoured by years of wildflowers. Honey sat down beside it and, for once,
moved slowly and carefully. The bees barely stirred.

He ate until the sun went down, licking every last drop from his paws,
and decided that perhaps patience was worth learning after all.
