Every Saturday, a boy named Eli walked to the top of Cutter's Hill with his red kite.

He had built the kite himself from two sticks of balsa wood, a sheet of red tissue paper,
and a ball of string his grandmother had given him. It was not a perfect kite — one side
pulled slightly to the left — but Eli had learned to compensate, holding the string at an angle
and letting the wind do the correcting.

At the top of the hill the wind was always strong. Eli would run three steps, let the string out,
and the kite would leap upward as if it had been waiting all week for exactly this moment.

He would stand there for hours, feeling the pull of the wind through the string, watching
the red shape grow smaller against the sky. When clouds came in from the west he could tell
by the kite's behaviour before he felt the change in the air — it would dip and shudder slightly,
as if nervous.

One afternoon in October the string snapped. He watched the red kite drift east,
growing smaller and smaller until it vanished beyond the treeline.

He walked home and began building a new one. He used orange tissue paper this time.
