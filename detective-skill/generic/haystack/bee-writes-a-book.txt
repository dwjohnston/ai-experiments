Beatrice was a bee who had always wanted to write a book.

The other bees thought this was peculiar. Bees made honey, not books. But Beatrice had
spent years flying from flower to flower, seeing things no one else had bothered to notice —
the way dew collected in the cup of a poppy at dawn, the secret paths between the lavender
and the clover, the old gardener who sang to his roses on Tuesday mornings.

She found a dry corner of the hive and began. Her legs were small and the pages
(borrowed from a fallen notepad in the garden) were enormous, but she was persistent.

She wrote about the garden through all four seasons. She wrote about the language of flowers —
how each one smelled different at noon than at dusk. She dedicated a whole chapter to rain.

When the book was finished she left it on the garden wall. A child found it the following morning.
The writing was tiny and smelled faintly of pollen, and the child could not read all of it,
but she pressed it carefully between two larger books and kept it for the rest of her life.
